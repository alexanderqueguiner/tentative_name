How Have the Sonic and Cultural Dimensions of Music Changed Over Time?

Alexander Queguiner1, Casey Tchoo1, Minjun Park1
1George Mason University Korea

Abstract

This project explores how the sound and cultural themes of popular music have changed over time using a dataset of songs released between 1950 and 2019. Each track contains metadata including release year, genre, lyrics-derived topics, and audio features. We visualize long-term patterns in genre dominance, acousticness, and explicit language. Our goal is to show how music reflects shifts in technology, culture, and artistic style.

Data and Methodology

We used the “Music Dataset 1950–2019” from Kaggle, which includes audio features (such as acousticness, loudness, energy, and valence) and topic scores extracted from lyrics. After cleaning release years and filtering missing values, we grouped songs by decade and genre. Visualizations were created in Python using pandas and matplotlib. A consistent dark-blue theme and fixed genre color palette were applied to all figures to ensure stylistic consistency across the website.

Results: Genre Trends and Cultural Signals

Below are three visualizations summarizing how genres evolve across decades, how acoustic they sound, and how explicit their lyrics tend to be. A shared horizontal legend ensures that colors remain consistent across the entire project.

Genre Dominance by Decade

This stacked bar chart reveals major shifts in genre popularity over time. Rock and Pop dominate earlier decades, whereas Hip hop rises significantly toward the 2000s and 2010s. Country, Reggae, and Blues remain smaller but stable contributors. The plot highlights how musical tastes diversify over time.

Acousticness Distribution by Genre

This boxplot compares how acoustic each genre tends to be. Country, Blues, and Jazz rely more on natural instrumentation, while Hip hop and Pop make heavier use of digital production. Rock falls in the middle, blending acoustic and electric elements.

Average Obscene Score by Genre

This bar chart highlights differences in explicit language across genres. Hip hop uses the most explicit language, followed by Rock and Pop. Jazz, Country, and Blues remain on the lower end, reflecting stylistic and cultural differences in lyrical expression.
